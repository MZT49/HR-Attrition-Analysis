# Recommendation Summary

## Finding
- Total workforce: 1,470 employees, 237 of whom left (16% overall attrition)
- Sales has the highest departmental attrition at 21%, while Sales 
  Representative is the highest-risk role at 39.8% nearly 1 in 2 leaving
- Employees working overtime leave at nearly 3x the rate of those who 
  don't (30.5% vs 10.4%)
- Leavers earned $4,787/month on average compared to $6,832/month for 
  retained employees, A $2,045 gap
- Attrition rate drops sharply as job satisfaction rises: 23% for low 
  satisfaction, 16% for medium, 11% for high

## Root Cause
- Overtime is the strongest single driver of attrition, pointing to 
  workload and burnout rather than isolated individual choices
- Compensation reinforces the problem - junior, lower-paid roles like 
  Sales Representative and Laboratory Technician combine high overtime 
  exposure with below-average pay, compounding the risk of leaving
- Low job satisfaction compounds both factors employees who are 
  overworked and underpaid are also the least satisfied, creating a 
  reinforcing cycle that pushes them toward leaving

## Recommendation
- **Review overtime policy for Sales and Laboratory roles** - these 
  combine the highest overtime exposure with the highest attrition; 
  reducing mandatory overtime or improving overtime compensation should 
  be the first intervention
- **Re-evaluate compensation for entry-level, high-risk roles** - 
  Sales Representatives and Laboratory Technicians are paid below the 
  company average yet carry disproportionate attrition risk
- **Investigate low-satisfaction segments directly** - conduct exit 
  interviews or satisfaction surveys focused on Sales and R&D departments 
  to identify specific dissatisfaction drivers beyond pay and hours
- **Prioritize retention efforts on junior roles** - since attrition is 
  concentrated in entry-level positions, retention investment will have 
  the highest return if focused there rather than spread evenly across 
  all roles
